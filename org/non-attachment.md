# Non-Attachment

## Definition

Non-attachment is the willingness to hold something — a person, an outcome, an identity, a desire — without needing it to stay the same. It is not the absence of care. It is the absence of *clinging*.

The distinction matters:

- **Detachment** withdraws. It stops caring to avoid the risk of loss. Non-attachment stays engaged but releases the grip on how things must turn out.
- **Apathy** doesn't want. Non-attachment can want intensely, but holds the wanting without requiring its satisfaction.
- **Indifference** doesn't prefer. Non-attachment has clear preferences, but can tolerate their disappointment without collapsing.

Non-attachment is the capacity to love, strive, and care fully *while* knowing that everything you love is temporary. It is commitment without hostage-taking.

## The Mechanism of Clinging

Clinging is not desire itself — it's desire plus the demand that desire be satisfied. The formula:

**Desire + demand = suffering**

The demand is the attachment: *this must happen, this must not end, this person must stay the same, I must be seen this way.* The desire is natural. The demand is the grip.

The practice is not to eliminate desire (which is neither possible nor desirable). It is to notice when desire has become demand, and to release the demand while keeping the desire.

## What Non-Attachment Applies To

### Outcomes
You can work toward an outcome with full effort while being okay if it doesn't arrive. The work is the thing you control; the outcome is not. Non-attachment to outcome is what sustains long effort — the activist, the builder, the caregiver — because the cost of every setback is lower when you haven't staked your existence on the result.

### Identity
You can hold a role — engineer, leader, parent — without fusing your self with it. When the role changes or ends, the person underneath remains. This is the central territory of [Being](being.md): not mistaking the mask for the face.

### People
You can love someone deeply without needing them to be who you want them to be. This is the hardest application. It's also the most liberating. The people who love you freely are not the ones who hold you to an image; they are the ones who are present to who you actually are, including who you become.

### The Self
You can let go of who you thought you were. The version of you from five years ago had different values, different fears, different understanding. Non-attachment to the fixed self is what allows growth without identity crisis.

## The Practice

Non-attachment is trained, not theorized:

**1. Notice the grip.** The next time you feel tight around an outcome — anxiety before a presentation, frustration when someone disagrees, disappointment when something doesn't go your way — pause. Feel the physical sensation of the grip. Where is it in your body? That's attachment.

**2. Name the demand.** Ask: *What am I requiring to be true for me to be okay?* "I need this deal to close." "I need them to see I was right." "I need this to stay the same." Say it aloud. The demand becomes visible.

**3. Release the demand, keep the desire.** You can still want the deal to close, still want to be understood, still want things to stay as they are. But you practice: *I want this. I am not required to have it.* The release is not resignation; it's returning to the ground of okay-ness that was always there.

**4. Return to what you can do.** Now act. Not from desperation or demand, but from the freedom of having released the outcome. The quality of action when you're not clinging is almost always better.
