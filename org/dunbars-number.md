# Dunbar's Number

## The Concept

In 1992, anthropologist Robin Dunbar published a finding that has quietly shaped how I think about organizations, governance, and the limits of human community: there is a cognitive ceiling of roughly **150 stable social relationships** that any individual human brain can maintain.

Not 150 acquaintances. 150 people whose identity, role, and trustworthiness you can track — people you know well enough to predict how they'll behave, to extend trust without a contract, to coordinate without a rulebook.

Above that threshold, informal social cohesion breaks down. You need formal rules, hierarchy, and enforcement — not because people become worse, but because the cognitive substrate for informal trust has been exceeded.

## The Social Brain Hypothesis

Dunbar's finding came from comparative neuroscience. Across primates, there's a robust correlation between neocortex size and typical social group size. The neocortex — the part of the brain responsible for social cognition, tracking relationships, modeling other minds — is the limiting factor. Bigger neocortex, larger stable group.

Extrapolating the regression line to human neocortex size predicts a group of ~150. And that number shows up everywhere:

- Average size of hunter-gatherer bands across cultures
- English village sizes in the Domesday Book
- The military company (the basic unit of infantry organization across most armies)
- Hutterite communities, which split when they exceed ~150 — not by rule, but because they discovered empirically that cohesion fails above it
- Gore-Tex factories, which cap their plants at 150 and open new ones rather than expand

The number has been confirmed by 23 independent studies of personal social networks, with a median sample of 5,457 individuals, across a wide range of cultures and historical periods. It's not a cultural artifact. It's a biological constraint.

## The Layered Structure

150 is the outer boundary, but Dunbar's research reveals a fractal structure of nested layers, each roughly 3x the previous:

| Layer | Size | Character |
|-------|------|-----------|
| Intimates | ~5 | Deep mutual support; you'd drop everything for these people |
| Trusted | ~15 | Close friends; regular contact, high emotional investment |
| Working | ~50 | Active collaborators; you know them well enough to work without friction |
| Community | ~150 | Stable social network; shared identity, informal trust |

Each layer has a qualitatively different character. The 5 are the people you call at 2am. The 15 are the people you'd invite to a small dinner. The 50 are the people you'd recognize at a conference and genuinely catch up with. The 150 are the people you'd help if they asked, without needing to know why.

These layers appear in digital networks, online gaming communities, and military organizations with the same ratios. The structure seems to be a property of how human social cognition is organized, not of any particular culture or medium.

## Why 150 Is a Threshold, Not Just a Number

Below 150, a community can run on informal trust. Everyone knows everyone's reputation. Social pressure and shared identity maintain norms. You don't need an HR department to handle disputes — the community handles it.

Above 150, that breaks. You can no longer hold the full social graph in your head. You can't know whether the person you're dealing with is trustworthy — you haven't built a relationship with them, and you don't know anyone who has. So you need formal rules, credentials, titles, and enforcement mechanisms to substitute for the trust that informal community would have provided.

This is why every organization that grows past ~150 people starts to feel different. The startup where everyone knew each other's names, where you could walk across the office and solve any problem informally — that's gone. Not because the people changed, but because the cognitive architecture for informal community has been exceeded.

## Personal Synthesis: The Org Scaling Problem

I've lived through this inflection point a few times. There's a specific moment — usually somewhere between 100 and 200 people — where you realize you don't know who to talk to about a problem anymore. The informal knowledge graph that let you navigate the organization has become too large to hold. You start needing org charts, Slack channels, documented processes — not because anyone wanted bureaucracy, but because the alternative is chaos.

As an IC, you can hold the social graph of your immediate team and a few adjacent ones. That's well within the 50-layer. You know who's good at what, who to ask, who to avoid. The work feels human-scale.

As a VP, you're operating at the 150-layer or beyond. You can't know everyone. You're managing through systems, culture, and delegation — not through direct relationship. The shift isn't just a change in scope; it's a change in the cognitive mode of leadership. You're no longer navigating a social graph you can hold. You're designing structures that work for people whose individual relationships you can't track.

The two-pizza team (5-10 people) is the real unit of high-trust, high-velocity work. Everything above that is coordination infrastructure.

## Implications for Governance and Human Structure

This is where Dunbar's number gets philosophically interesting.

If 150 is the natural ceiling for a trust-based community — the scale at which humans can coordinate without formal rules — then every larger human structure is, in some sense, a workaround for a cognitive limitation.

**Nation-states** require constitutions, laws, courts, and police not because humans are inherently untrustworthy, but because the social graph is too large for informal trust to operate. The formal apparatus substitutes for the relationship knowledge that would exist in a 150-person community.

**Democracy at scale** is a Dunbar workaround. We can't know our representatives personally, so we use proxies — party affiliation, platform, reputation, endorsements — to simulate the trust judgments we'd make naturally in a smaller community. The dysfunction of large-scale democracy is partly a story about how badly those proxies work compared to actual relationship knowledge.

**The most resilient human structures** may be federations of ~150-person units rather than monolithic hierarchies. The Hutterites figured this out empirically. So did the Roman legion (the century, ~80 men, nested into cohorts of ~480). The structure that scales isn't the one that grows without limit — it's the one that replicates at the natural unit size.

**Moloch as a Dunbar failure**: Many coordination failures that [Meditations on Moloch](meditations-on-moloch-summary.md) describes are partly a story about scale. Informal trust and shared identity can hold a community together against defection. But when the group exceeds 150, those mechanisms fail. You can't shame someone you don't know. You can't appeal to shared identity with someone who doesn't share your community. The tragedy of the commons is, in part, a tragedy of groups too large for informal coordination.

The implication isn't that we should only live in villages of 150. It's that when we build larger structures, we should be honest about what we're doing: we're constructing formal substitutes for cognitive capacities that don't scale. And we should design those substitutes with that limitation in mind — keeping the units of actual trust and collaboration small, even when the formal organization is large.
