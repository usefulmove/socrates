# On Accountability and Its Enemies

## The Freyd Observation

Jennifer Freyd is a psychologist whose research on betrayal trauma — the particular harm done when the person who abuses you is also the person you depend on — led her to a related observation about how abusers respond when held accountable.

> "I hypothesize that if an accusation is true, and the accused person is abusive, the denial is more indignant, self-righteous and manipulative, as compared with denial in other cases. Similarly, I have observed that actual abusers threaten, bully and make a nightmare for anyone who holds them accountable or asks them to change their abusive behavior. This attack, intended to chill and terrify, typically includes threats. ... The attack will often take the form of focusing on ridiculing the person who attempts to hold the offender accountable."
>
> — Jennifer Freyd, 1997

The clinical observation has a sharp edge: the *intensity* of the denial is itself diagnostic. Innocent people protest innocence. Abusers do something different — they go on the offensive against whoever is doing the accounting. The goal isn't to establish the truth; it's to make the cost of holding them accountable high enough that people stop.

## DARVO

Freyd later named the pattern she was describing: **DARVO** — Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim and Offender. The accused:

1. **Denies** the behavior
2. **Attacks** the person making the accusation
3. **Reverses** the roles — positions themselves as the real victim, the accuser as the aggressor

This isn't unique to clinical abuse contexts. It shows up in organizations, in politics, in families. Anywhere power is asymmetric and accountability is unwelcome.

The pattern is recognizable once you know to look for it:
- The person called out for aggressive behavior who responds by accusing the caller-out of hostility
- The leader whose team raises concerns who responds by questioning the team's loyalty
- The colleague who misses deliverables who responds to feedback by cataloguing your failures
- The public figure accused of misconduct who immediately launches a campaign to discredit the accuser

The reversal is the tell. Not just "I didn't do it" but "you're the one doing harm by saying I did."

---

## Why This Matters for Leadership

Holding people accountable is a core leadership function. It's also one of the most emotionally costly. The DARVO pattern is one reason why.

When you give someone hard feedback and they respond with indignant counter-attack — questioning your motives, your competence, your fairness — the psychological pressure is real. You start second-guessing yourself. Maybe I was too harsh. Maybe I missed context. Maybe the problem is how I raised it, not what I raised.

This self-doubt is often appropriate — good feedback requires humility about your own blind spots. But DARVO weaponizes that self-doubt. The goal is to make you so focused on defending your process that you drop the original concern.

A few things that help:
- **Note the pattern explicitly** (to yourself, or to a trusted colleague): *Is this a genuine engagement with the substance, or a deflection?*
- **Separate the meta-conversation from the original one**: You can acknowledge that the feedback could have been delivered differently while not abandoning the substance of it
- **Look at what's missing**: A genuine response to accountability addresses the actual behavior. DARVO never does.
- **Calibrate the intensity of the response**: Defensive discomfort is normal. Intense, organized counter-attack — especially one targeting your credibility rather than the facts — is a different thing.

---

## The Limits

Freyd's observation is a hypothesis about a pattern, not a diagnostic rule. Not everyone who responds to an accusation with indignation is guilty. Not every counter-attack is DARVO. High-stakes false accusations exist and they produce intense, justified responses.

The pattern is useful as a flag, not a verdict. If someone's response to being held accountable seems more focused on destroying the accountability attempt than on engaging with the substance — that's worth noticing. What you do with that noticing depends on everything else you know.
