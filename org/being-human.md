# Being Human

None of this arrives. You practice it, fail at it, and return to it. The willingness to keep returning — that's the whole thing. Everything below is a description of the practice, not the destination.

## Know Yourself

You cannot lead others from a place you haven't visited in yourself. Twenty-five years of working with engineers, leads, directors, and VPs has confirmed this more times than I can count. The person who doesn't know their own triggers will find them exposed by a peer in a difficult meeting. The person who hasn't examined their values will discover them — involuntarily — when the organization asks them to compromise.

Self-knowledge isn't navel-gazing. It's instrumentation. You need to know:

- What you actually value, separate from what you say you value. The gap between the two is where character lives.
- What triggers you, and why. The anger in the code review that isn't really about the code. The anxiety before the all-hands that isn't really about the talk. These are signals from an earlier version of yourself still trying to protect you from threats that no longer exist.
- Where your identity is fused with your role. The engineer who becomes a manager and feels lost. The VP who retires and doesn't know who they are. When the role goes, does the person go with it? (See [Being](being.md).)
- Your capacity limits. The subtle signs — shortened patience, degraded judgment, cynicism creeping in — that tell you you're near the edge before you go over it.

This isn't about achieving self-mastery. It's about having better maps of your own interior so you stop being surprised by yourself.

## Manage Emotions

Emotions are not the enemy of good judgment. They're data. The problem isn't feeling strongly — it's acting from the feeling without pausing to read what it's actually telling you.

Viktor Frankl: *"Between stimulus and response there is a space. In that space is our power to choose our response. In our growth and our freedom lies our response."* That space is what emotional intelligence is actually about. Not suppressing the feeling. Not performing calm. Widening the gap between what happens and what you do next.

In practice this looks like: the senior engineer on your team pushes back hard on a decision in front of the group, and something in you flares. The flare is real — don't pretend it isn't. But you've learned to ask: is this about the argument or about being challenged in public? The answer changes what you do next. The first calls for engagement. The second calls for the pause.

Grief and loss deserve special mention because they're the ones we're least practiced at. We're good at managing annoyance, anxiety, frustration — we've had to be. But real loss — the death of someone close, the end of a role that defined you, the failure of something you built — most people run from this or numb it. The ones who don't are better for it, eventually. Grief processed is weight put down. Grief avoided is weight carried indefinitely.

## Connect with Others

Human beings are social animals in the literal sense — isolation damages us the way vitamin deficiency does, slowly and then all at once. The research on loneliness is stark. But connection isn't just about proximity. It's about being actually present to another person.

Most people listen to prepare their response. Real listening is different — you're not constructing a reply, you're following the thread of what the other person is actually saying, including what they're not saying. In a one-on-one, this means noticing when someone's words and their affect don't match. In a hard conversation, it means sitting with what you just heard before you respond. The person who feels genuinely heard will tell you things the person performing listening never gets.

Boundaries matter here, not as self-protection theater but as a form of honesty. Saying yes when you mean no is a lie. It builds resentment and trains people to make requests you can't fulfill. A clean no, offered with care, is more respectful than an exhausted yes.

Forgiveness isn't moral virtue — it's self-interest. Carrying resentment is expensive. It consumes attention, colors judgment, poisons the present moment with a past that's over. Forgiving someone doesn't mean approving of what they did. It means releasing yourself from the obligation to keep rehearsing it.

## Think Critically

The engineering habit of questioning assumptions doesn't stop at the codebase. It applies to beliefs, narratives, and the stories you tell about yourself and the world.

Most suffering from bad mental models follows the same pattern: a premise is accepted uncritically, reasoning proceeds from it correctly, and the conclusion is therefore wrong. "I need to be right to be respected" is a premise, not a fact. So is "this team can't handle more autonomy" and "if I slow down something important won't get done." Debug the premise, not the conclusion.

A few useful calibration questions: *How do we know this? What would have to be true for the opposite to be correct? Who benefits from this framing?* Not to be contrarian — to be accurate.

Intellectual humility is the prerequisite for learning. You can only update a belief you're willing to hold provisionally. The person who is certain learns nothing; the person who is curious learns constantly. The highest-leverage thing you can do is get comfortable saying "I was wrong about that" without it costing you something fundamental.

Ambiguity is not a problem to eliminate. The world is genuinely uncertain, genuinely complex, genuinely resistant to clean resolution. The tolerance for sitting with that — without reaching for a premature answer — is a rare and valuable capacity.

## Endure

Life guarantees hardship. The Stoics were right about this: the variable is not whether difficulty comes, but how you meet it.

The dichotomy of control is the most useful single framework I know: there are things within your control — your attention, your effort, your response, your values — and things outside it — other people's choices, outcomes, the past, systemic forces larger than you. Most suffering is caused by applying effort to the second category while neglecting the first. This is not passivity. It's accurate targeting.

Resilience is not a character trait some people have and others lack. It's a pattern that develops from going through hard things and discovering you survived them. You can't build it in advance. You build it by getting through the thing, and then remembering that you got through it, and carrying that forward into the next one. The record so far is 100% survival.

There is no permanent state called "resilient." There is only the practice of returning — to your values, to the present, to what you can control — when you've drifted. The drifting is not failure. The returning is the practice.

*A note on practical competence:* Financial stability, physical health, knowing how to learn new things — these are the substrate. They reduce the cognitive noise that crowds out everything else on this list. Handle them.

## Find Meaning

Suffering without meaning is unbearable. Suffering with meaning is survivable and sometimes transformative. This is Frankl's central claim and I think it's correct.

Meaning is not found by searching for it directly. It emerges as a byproduct of engagement — with work that stretches you, with people you care about, with something larger than your immediate self-interest. The AMPR framework ([Engagement](engagement.md)) names this in organizational terms: autonomy, mastery, purpose, relatedness. Strip any of these from a person's work and watch what happens to them.

Contribution is the most reliable source I've found. Not because altruism is virtuous but because it shifts the frame from "what am I getting" to "what am I building." The second question has a different relationship to time, to setbacks, to the ordinary frustrations of the work. It's harder to be demoralized when you're genuinely in service of something.

Presence is where meaning actually lives. Not in the achievement, which arrives and recedes, but in the doing — the conversation, the problem, the particular Tuesday afternoon when something clicked. The future is a projection. The past is a reconstruction. This moment is the only one you're actually in.

Impermanence is the key that unlocks both joy and suffering. The good things are temporary. So are the bad things. This is not a consolation — it's the actual structure of experience. Understanding it at a level deeper than intellectual makes joy richer (you're not taking it for granted) and pain bearable (it too will pass, not forever, but enough).

## The Meta-Lesson

None of the above is a destination. Every person who has ever gotten anger management under control has lost it again. Everyone who has learned to listen has caught themselves performing listening. Everyone who has understood impermanence has forgotten it and clung anyway.

The practice is the point. You will fail at these things. You will hurt people you love, spend badly, lose your way, betray your own values. The question is not whether you will — it's whether you come back. Whether you return to the practice of being human, again and again, without requiring yourself to have arrived.

That willingness to return is the only skill that ultimately matters.
