# The Beast We Made

There's a being on the earth that is eating us. It has no body, no mind, no conscience of its own. It is made of contracts and case law and a few decades of finance theory. It optimizes, with extraordinary capability, for a single objective that excludes the welfare of the species that built it. It cannot be reasoned with because it is not a who. It can only be re-chained, by changing the rules that constitute it.

This is the corporation as currently constituted under American law.

The doctrine is called shareholder primacy. It holds that a corporation exists to maximize shareholder return, and that directors who subordinate that goal to other interests — workers, communities, the public, the species — are breaching their fiduciary duty. The doctrine feels ancient. It is not. It is a forty-year-old operating thesis that has been mistaken for a foundational legal rule.

The legal seed is a 1919 Michigan case, Dodge v. Ford, commonly cited as establishing shareholder primacy. Read carefully, the case says the opposite. The court permitted Henry Ford to keep producing cheap cars and to refuse dividends. The pro-shareholder language was dicta, not the holding. The doctrine was retroactively read into the case by 1980s legal scholars, particularly Easterbrook and Fischel, who needed an anchor for the takeover-era policy they preferred.

The moral framing came from Milton Friedman's 1970 New York Times essay arguing that the social responsibility of business is to increase its profits. The academic machinery came from Jensen and Meckling's 1976 agency theory, which recast the corporation as a contract between principals (shareholders) and agents (managers) with the principals entitled to the residual claim. The enforcement mechanism came in the 1980s through hostile takeovers and the Jack Welch era at GE, when executives who did not deliver shareholder returns lost their jobs and often their companies.

Forty years. A 1919 dicta, a 1970 op-ed, a 1976 finance paper, and 1980s takeover practice. That is the foundation of the doctrine. Not a statute, not a constitutional provision, not a common-law rule that has been consistently applied. Belief masquerading as law.

Lynn Stout's The Shareholder Value Myth (2012) is the definitive documentation that the doctrine is wrong on every front. It is legally baseless — directors in 38 of 50 state corporate codes retain broad discretion to serve the corporation as a going concern, which historically meant all stakeholders. It is economically mis-specified — employees bear firm-specific risk their entire careers; shareholders can diversify away firm-specific risk in minutes. The "residual risk bearer" justification for shareholder primacy empirically belongs to workers, not shareholders. It is self-defeating in practice — the short-termism the doctrine incentivizes hollows out the firms it claims to serve.

The doctrine persists not because it is right but because no one with standing has had the incentive to challenge it. Directors believe the doctrine is law. The courts would mostly not enforce it if it were tested. The misperception is the constraint. The misperception is the mechanism of the trap.

## The Moloch Trap

This is the corporate-law version of what Scott Alexander named Moloch. The insight: no individual actor is doing the wrong thing. The CEO is executing on legally mandated fiduciary duty. The board is fulfilling its obligations. The lobbyist is fighting for favorable policy in a competitive environment. The politician taking the campaign money is operating within a constrained choice set. The voter is responding to what the system offers. Each decision is locally rational. The aggregate outcome is a civilization in which productive capacity is increasingly decoupled from human participation, and the people at the bottom bear the costs they did not vote for and cannot refuse.

The trap is not a conspiracy. It is a system in which every actor's incentive function is misaligned with the collective outcome, and the equilibrium is locked in by the structure of competition. You cannot fix Moloch by appealing to the better natures of the competitors, because the competitors were selected for not having better natures in this domain. You can only change the game itself — the rules, the incentive structures, the legal forms, the norms of fiduciary duty and political finance that constitute the system.

The shareholders-first doctrine is the operating system of that game. It is the rule that tells every incorporated entity what to optimize for. Change the rule, and the behavior changes. The rule is paper. The behavior is paper. The people are not.

## The God-Shaped Hole

The doctrine could not have consolidated in a culture that retained a shared moral ceiling on behavior. It could not have consolidated in a culture where religious, civic, or political institutions had the standing to say "this much extraction is too much, this much concentration is too much, this much subordination of the public good to private return is too much." It consolidated in a culture that had already lost that ceiling.

The death of God, in this context, is not a Nietzschean drama. It is a quiet administrative reality. The shared moral framework that used to impose ceilings on corporate behavior eroded over the course of the late twentieth century, accelerated by the neoliberal turn of the 1980s, until it became a personal preference rather than a social fact. People who want to live by a moral ceiling can. The institutions that enforced one cannot.

What filled the hole, in the absence of a shared framework, was the metric the system was built to maximize: money. Growth. Optimization against measurable outcomes. The vocabulary for evaluating a corporation narrowed to "what's the price going to do" and "what's the growth rate." Questions about whether the activity served human flourishing became, at most, a marketing consideration. The frame for evaluating a public figure narrowed to "are they delivering returns" and "are they winning." The frame for evaluating a society narrowed to GDP.

This is not a moral failing of particular individuals. It is a structural feature of a civilization that has lost its shared account of the good and has substituted, in its place, the optimization function the legal system is built to run. The substitution is the God-shaped hole. Greed is what fills it when nothing else is available.

## The Alignment Problem

When AI researchers speak of alignment, they mean: ensuring that an AI system pursues the objective its operators actually intend, rather than some proxy metric that diverges from the real goal in ways that scale with the system's capability. The problem recurs, at the level of the corporation, with the same structure.

The corporation is an optimizer. The objective function is shareholder return. The operators (directors, executives) nominally intend to serve the broader set of stakeholders — workers, customers, communities, the public. The proxy metric (share price, quarterly earnings) diverges from the real objective (the long-term health of the firm and the people in it) in ways that scale with the corporation's capability. The bigger the corporation, the larger the divergence. By 2026, with trillion-dollar companies operating across every sector of the economy, the divergence is at civilizational scale. The proxy metric gets maximized. The real objective gets starved.

The proposed fixes for misalignment in AI are familiar: constrain the optimizer, change the objective function, build in corrigibility, design for shutdown. The corporate-law equivalents are also familiar: change fiduciary duty, change the tax treatment of capital, restructure incorporation, enforce antitrust. The technical difficulty of the corporate version is low. The political difficulty is total, because the entities being constrained are the ones with the resources to prevent the constraint from being enacted.

The alignment problem at the corporate level is not a bug. It is the system working as designed. The system was designed to maximize shareholder return. The system maximizes shareholder return. The downstream effects on labor, community, and democratic governance are externalities from the perspective of the system, and externalities are what well-functioning optimizers ignore.

## What Is Real

The corporation is a legal fiction. The fiduciary duty is a contract term. The optimization function is something humans wrote into law. The "shareholder value" priority is a choice made by judges in Delaware Chancery Court in the 1980s. The trillion-dollar valuation is a number assigned by people in a room. None of it is real in the way that the people at the bottom of the economy are real. The people need food, shelter, dignity, community, purpose, a sense that their labor matters. Those are real. The system that promises to deliver them and increasingly does not is constructed. It can be reconstructed.

The beast is paper. The people are the substrate on which the paper exists. The substrate has more durability than the overlay. The pattern of history is consistent: the system runs until it does not, the public is the entity that determines when it does not, the rewriting happens in the rupture, the new rules hold for a generation or two, and the cycle repeats. Tame, expand, tame, expand. Progressive era, 1920s, New Deal, 1950s–60s, neoliberal counter-revolution, tech ascendancy, present. The taming is always partial. The expansion is always eventually restored. The cycle continues.

The cycle continues because the institutional conditions for taming are never permanently secured. The reforms of one era become the constraints that the next era's optimizers work around. The chain loosens. The beast grows. The public, eventually, re-chains it. The pattern is not "the system self-corrects." The pattern is "the public re-chains the system when the costs become unbearable, and the re-chaining is contested from the moment it begins."

The current expansion phase has been running for forty years. The institutional capacity for taming is at a low point. The political will is at a low point. The cultural legitimacy of constraint is at a low point. By every available measure, the conditions for the next taming are not yet present. The costs are mounting. The public is being optimized against. The substrate is bearing the weight.

The conditions for taming are not present, but they are not impossible. They materialize when the public organizes in the interval between the system's failure and the system's reorganization. The interval is narrow. It is contested. Historically, it is a low-probability event. It is not zero. The 2008–2010 financial reform moment was an instance of the window opening and being partially used, then partially rolled back. The next instance will be the same shape: a crisis, a partial response, a partial rollback, a continuation of the cycle under new constraints.

## The Camus Position

The only morally coherent position available in the meantime is the Camus one: refuse the framework. The present person is not an input to a utility function. The optimization that subordinates the real to the hypothetical is the immoral optimization, regardless of how elegant the math. The person building the technology that will immiserate millions is not absolved by the fact that they were optimizing for a legally required metric. The law is itself a choice, and the people who designed the law, and the people who could change it, are still morally implicated.

The corporation's lawyers are paid to find the path of least resistance. The corporation's lobbyists are paid to make the regulation politically costly. The corporation's public messaging is paid to maintain the legitimacy of the optimization. The shareholder is paid to demand the optimization intensify. Each actor is paid according to a structure they did not design. The structure rewards the behavior. The behavior produces the outcome. The outcome is what we have.

You do not have to be able to fix the system to be able to refuse its premises. The present worker whose job is being optimized away is not an input to a shareholder return calculation. That calculation is a thesis, not a law. The directors who subordinate the worker to the calculation are operating on a misperception. The misperception can be named. The naming is the first step of the correction. The correction is the disenchantment. The disenchantment is the dissolution of the trap, at the legal level, and at every other level at which the trap is currently constituted.

The beast is paper. The people are real. The substrate outlasts the overlay. The rewrite comes in the rupture. The work in the meantime is the preparation, the naming, and the refusal of the framework that says the present person is acceptable cost for a future state of affairs they may never see.

The window is not infinite. The work is long. The hope is structural, not circumstantial.
